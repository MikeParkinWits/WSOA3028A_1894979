Should you be worried about your Privacy in the Digital Age?

By Michael Parkin | 18 June 2021 | Words: 549 | Theory

In the modern day, almost everyone has a story about how their device must be listening in on their conversations. But why is this, and should users then be worried about their privacy? In this blog post I explore this concern, why it happens, and whether users should be worried about their privacy.

To start, we must look at what the concern is. Most people have heard numerous stories from both friends, and family, about how they met up with a friend and discussed something, and soon after that the very thing they were discussing appears on their phone as an advert – whether it be a holiday location, clothing item, or a restaurant. This often leaves people with a sour taste in their mouth, as they believe their privacy has been breached. To make things worse, there have been multiple reports of big tech companies, such as Facebook and Amazon, listening in on saved conversations. This has led to a slew of creative options for protecting one’s privacy, such as covering microphones and webcams with tape. But is there truly a need for this, or is it nothing more than just an unnecessary precaution?

The simple answer is that these big companies don’t actually need access to your microphone, or camera, to serve you these ads. In 2018, the world learnt of the data analysis company Cambridge Analytica, which had been collecting and analyzing data from millions of Facebook users all around the world. While this company has since gone bankrupt, it gave users an insight into just how much data they give up online. In fact, it was found that big companies have almost 5 000 data points on every person in the United States alone, all from information that users have shared freely online.

But how do you get adverts for items you never searched but merely discussed then? Robert Reeve, a privacy tech worker, explains this. He says that these data points are everywhere, not just in what you search but in anything that can be linked to you in some way online – ranging from rewards programs with your email, which track your buying habits; or even your proximity to your friends, which allows for their search habits to be cross referenced with yours. As such, all this data is both more openly available, and easier to analyse, when compared to any data that could come from your microphone.

But what does this mean for a user’s privacy? While I still believe that users should be concerned about their privacy, they must direct this concern towards the data they are willingly giving away by accepting cookies, and privacy policies, without understanding them. Luckily, however, this has been changing in recent years – with companies such as Google and Apple adopting an opt-in approach to sharing data, as opposed to opting out.

In conclusion, I looked at why users believe their device is listening to them, and how this is not true. Instead, I showed that big companies do not need to do this, as they collect thousands of data points to target ads towards you. However, I concluded by noting that while users must still be cautious of the information they share, big tech companies are starting to change this and put user’s privacy at the forefront.
