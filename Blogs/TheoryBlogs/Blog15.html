Exploring Metaphors and Meaning Making in Digital Spaces

By Michael Parkin | 7 May 2021 | Words: 498 | Theory

Metaphors have become so engrained in how we process information digitally that we have become unaware that they are even used. In this blog post I explore the use of metaphors in the digital space, looking at their origin and how we still use and interact with them today – using examples throughout to demonstrate this.

At the beginning of the digital age, when technology was becoming more common place, most consumers were skeptical about using it as it appeared as too complicated. Engineers and software developers then had to find ways to counter this in order to allow technology to expand beyond just the enthusiast realm. As such, the use of metaphors was employed to give people something to relate to in digital spaces. Early examples of this can be seen on the internet, where terms such as ‘cyberspace’ and ‘superhighway’ were coined to teach users about what the internet is, and how it functions.

Although, terms like ‘cyberspace’ and ‘superhighway’ are rarely used in the modern day, as most users understand the idea of the internet now, which brings about the question: are metaphors even still used in digital spaces today? The answer to this is a resounding yes, with most people unaware that they interact with these metaphors daily as they have become so ubiquitous – the most obvious metaphors found in user interfaces. User interface and user experience designers use interaction metaphors – which are metaphors that communicate the result of interactions – to increase the overall user experience. By doing this the designer can easily: describe patterns, reducing confusion about how to perform sequential actions; communicate the purpose of an element, and how to interact with it; and lastly relate items to the real world in order to create easier association for the user.

To illustrate we can look at ecommerce websites. Almost all these websites make use of various symbols and terms that are similar to physical commerce stores. Users will interact with ‘baskets’, or ‘shopping carts’, to store the items they want to purchase, and once they are done shopping they can then click a button to take them to the ‘checkout’ in order to pay. So, although users never actually place anything in a basket, nor physically proceed to the checkout, these metaphors help to create a real-world association that assists the user in understanding how to interact with the core of the website. However, this is just one example, with metaphors being used in every digital space – from design languages such as skeuomorphism, which is about creating interface objects which mimic their real-world counterpart, all the way to explaining complex topics such as net neutrality.

In conclusion, I looked at how metaphors came to be used in digital spaces. On top of this, I look at interaction metaphors in the modern day, using the example of ecommerce websites and their ‘baskets’ and ‘checkout’ to illustrate this. However, I also note that metaphors span much further than this and touch all aspects of digital space.
