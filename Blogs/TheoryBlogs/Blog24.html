The Problem with Social Media Algorithms

By Michael Parkin | 16 June 2021 | Words: 575 | Theory

Social media has been around for most of the internet era, and like the internet, it has changed drastically over the past few decades. Originally, social media was used to connect with friends and family but, through the use of algorithms, it now acts as a space where users explore relevant information curated just for them. This, in turn, sparked questions around the algorithm’s used by social media – such as a Twitter thread by Marc Faddoul, questioning the ethical nature of TikTok’s algorithm. As such, in this blog post I look at the issues raised in this Twitter thread, evaluating the use of algorithms in social media, and the problems that arise from them.

As stated in the introduction, the purpose of social media has shifted over the years. This shift was part of a move from organically curated content, where users only see content linearly from accounts they follow, to algorithmically curated content, which revolves around keeping users on the platform by serving them more relevant content. While each social media platform is different, with their own unique algorithms, the reason behind this change revolved around accommodating spam and the growing amount of content, allowing for a more pleasant user experience.

However, these algorithms brought a handful of problems. As seen in the screenshot below, Marc Faddoul identifies a clear bias in TikTok’s algorithm. He states that when a user tries to follow someone on TikTok, the platform automatically recommends other users with similar appearances – not only in ethnicity and/or gender, but also in hair colour, visible disabilities, body proportions, and more.

This creates the assumption that TikTok purposefully built an algorithm to group users based on physical appearance, as opposed to content. However, through further research this is quickly debunked, as multiple users have confirmed TikTok’s claims that they use collaborative filtering – an algorithm that recommends users to follow based on other user’s follower habits. But why do recommended users all have similar appearances? This falls into the realm of human bias, showing that users have an inherent bias by following people with certain appearances. The algorithm then carries these biases forward, ultimately leading to the algorithm having an appearance bias of its own.

Although, the biggest ethical concerns revolve around the feedback loop that this algorithm creates. If users are only ever recommended similair users to follow, their bias is further reinforced, creating a perpetual loop wherein all users are served the same, bias, content. If most users also have this bias, then the platform ultimately favours this – discouraging any diversity. Unfortunately, this behaviour is not unique to TikTok, and can be seen in most social media platforms. As a result, any inherent human bias that exists can be perpetuated by the very algorithms that have been created to try and alleviate them. This is a problem, as developers believe that they are creating an unbiased algorithm, when in fact they are doing the opposite. Therefore, it is imperative that developers understand the consequences of what they create, so that they can create around these ethical issues.

In conclusion, by using TikTok as an example, I looked at the algorithms that are present in social media. I then evaluated how these algorithms create an inherent bias on various social media platforms, raising ethical concerns around the use of algorithms in social media. On top of this, I also discussed why developers should take this human bias into account, and design around them.
