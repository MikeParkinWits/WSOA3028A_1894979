The Library of Babel and the Internet

By Michael Parkin | March 12th 2021 | Words: 613 | Theory

In this blog post I reflect on the 1944 work The Library of Babel by Jorge Luis Borges. Although this story predates the internet, many theorists believe that the library that Borges describes directly relates to the internet. To start I go over the story’s main concepts and ideas before comparing it to the concepts of the internet, and I finish by stating whether I believe the story and its concepts should be compared at all.

Firstly, in its most basic form The Library of Babel presents the reader with the idea that there is a vast, almost infinite, library that consists of every book ever written. However, this library is only infinite because all the books consist of every possible permutation of a specific set of letters, and punctuation – whether the books make logical sense or not. This is Borges attempt at creating an ideal and universal library that consists of all of mankind’s knowledge.

This idea of a library that is infinite and consists of all mankind’s knowledge is the reason that most theorists compare the library of Babel to the internet, and it is important that we compare the extent of their infiniteness. While Borges originally states that the library is infinite, he later contradicts himself and states that “each book contains four hundred ten pages; each page, forty lines; each line, approximately eighty black letters.” (Borges & Hurley, 1999, pp. 113) This would limit the numbers of books, although to a very large number. Compared to the internet, a similar idea can be seen. People refer to the internet as infinite, not because it is truly infinite but because it is not feasible to account for all of it as it is ever growing. While one could argue that the internet is truly infinite and continually expanding, I would argue that it is not. If one applied the same algorithm from the library of Babel, they would be required to include all the different variables that the internet consists of, which would result in a number that, while huge, is not infinite.

Furthermore, one can also make a connection between the library of Babel and the internet because of the uniqueness of the information in both. The library of Babel consists of unique texts, that potentially only differ slight, but are still unique. This can also be seen on the internet through webpages and Uniform Resource Locators (URLs). Although most webpages that are commonly visited are distinct and unique, there are inevitably webpages that are identical in every way. However, their URLs would have to differ, even if by just a single letter – just like the books in the library of Babel.

Lastly, one can look at the content of the books in the library of Babel. Borges states that “for every rational line or forthright statement there are leagues of senseless cacophony, verbal nonsense, and incoherency.” (Borges & Hurley, 1999, pp. 114) This proves the point that most books in the library contain gibberish and are undecipherable, requiring a person to sift through mostly pointless information in search of knowledge. The same can be said about the internet, not because most information is gibberish but instead because users of it have to sift through worthless information, such as fake news, in order to find knowledge.

In conclusion, taking into account that this story predates the invention of the internet, it is impressive that Borges was able to refer to concepts that would only come decades later. As a result, I definitely agree with theorists who believe that the library of Babel can be compared to the internet, as long as one keeps the date of publication in mind.

References
