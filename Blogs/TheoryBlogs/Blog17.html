The Digital Divide in South Africa

By Michael Parkin | 21 May 2021 | Words: 390 | Theory

In today’s day and age, access to Information and Communication Technologies (ICTs) is crucial however, access to ICTs is not always available to everyone – this results in a digital divide. In this blog post I discuss this digital divide in the context of South Africa, looking specifically at the reasoning behind it and how this divide can be bridged.

First, it is important to understand what the digital divide is, and the reasons behind it both globally and in South Africa. As stated in the introduction, a digital divide occurs when there is an unequal access to ICTs. According to a research survey done in 2013 (Zickuhr & Smith, 2013), there are five factors globally that result in the global divide: a lack of access to ICTs, the cost associated with accessing these ICTs, a lower education level, a lower technical and digital literacy, and lastly a lack of actual interest in going digital. While these factors play a huge role in the reasoning behind the digital divide in South Africa, they have ultimately stemmed from Apartheid era inequality which continues to have a lasting effect on all aspects of inequality in South Africa. This is extremely worrying as more and more aspects of our lives become digital, especially in the wake of the global pandemic, people continue to be left behind – in turn increasing the digital divide.

As a result, it is crucial that we work quickly to try and counter act this divide and the lasting effects of previous inequalities. To achieve this, there are two main areas of focus. First, it is important that there is funding and investment in these areas to help create access to ICTs in areas that need it the most – whether through free internet hotspots or free beginner computer courses. Secondly, it is important that higher education produces graduates that are extremely technically and digitally able. This increase in technically able people would in turn allow for greater technical competency in all aspects of the country.

In conclusion, the digital divide is a huge problem for not only the world, but specifically South Africa, as the divide stems from Apartheid-era inequalities. However, not all hope is lost as with a focus on investment in digital infrastructure and digital competency at higher education levels, we can see a future where these is a much smaller digital divide.
