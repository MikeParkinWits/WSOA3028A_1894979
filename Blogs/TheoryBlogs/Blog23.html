Critcally analysing my Websites UI and UX Design

By Michael Parkin | 14 June 2021 | Words: 560 | Theory

User interface (UI), and user experience (UX), design are extremely important when developing a website, yet so often they are merely just an afterthought. This is problematic, as bad UI and UX instantly result in a bad site – no matter how good the content is. As such, in this blog post I critically analyse both my websites UI and UX. More specifically, I will start by defining each term, before moving on to look at the UI and UX I have implemented, critically reflecting on my decisions.

Firstly, we will start by looking at my website’s user experience. To do this, however, we must understand what UX is. UX is all about the functionality of the website, and the experience the user gets from using it – looking specifically at how users experience the site, and how easily they can perform the task they set out to achieve.

With that, we can then start to analyse my websites UX. Throughout its creation, I made a point of keeping the user, and their needs, in mind. As a result, there were two questions that I asked myself: what are users trying to achieve when visiting the website, and is the process as easy as possible? Thus, I established two potential goals for users visiting my site – learning more about my fictional character, Jake Sky, and his photography; or looking for my blogs. When a user first lands on my website they are presented with the homepage, where I accommodate for these two user goals in three ways: presenting the user with an example of my fictional characters photography, giving quick access to the characters most recent portfolio piece, and lastly giving access to my three most recent blog posts. Using these, I make the users experience as fluid as possible. However, in the case that the user has other goals, a persistent navigation bar, with clearly labelled items, is present – allowing for easy navigate across the entire site.

In addition to this, there is the website’s UI. In general, UI consists of the visual design of the website – encompassing its look, presentation, and interactivity. This can most easily be identified through a websites colour choice, buttons, fonts, and more.

For my UI, I chose to mainly rely on a minimalistic design, and colour scheme, for multiple reasons. Firstly, it is imperative that the site portray Jakes personality, as he is a minimalist. Secondly, this minimalist design, and colour scheme, help to put a focus on the content of the website. This is important, as the main purpose of the site is to showcase Jakes photography. By using a clean user interface, and grayscale, both Jakes photography, and all other content across the site, are highlighted and accentuated. Lastly, it is important to note that this minimalist design was not done at the expense of usability. In fact, usability is at the core of the UI. Sitewide, users are given adequate indicators about the function of multiple elements – ranging from cards ‘lifting up’, buttons inverting their colours, and the navbar items changing shades when a user hovers over them to indicate they are interactable.

In conclusion, by first defining UI and UX, I was able to critically analyse my sites design. However, with a longer blog post I could have gone into more depth, evaluating more UI/UX elements – such as consistency and imagery.
