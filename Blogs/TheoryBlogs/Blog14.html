Information Geographies - A Critical Reflection

By Michael Parkin | 30 April 2021 | Words: 1132 | Theory

The distribution of information geographically has long been augmented, with the Global North being the main producer of information over the Global South. However, with the dawn of the internet many believed that this new access to information would shift this geography of information. In particular, journal articles such as “Towards a study of information geographies” – by Mark Graham, Stefano De Sabbata, and Matthew Zook (2015) – use different metrics of information to evaluate whether the internet age has created equal geographies of information in the 21st century. As such, in this blog post I look more closely at this journal article by first summarising the article through the same three categories of geographic distribution that the article uses: geographies of access, participation, and representation. I then conclude by reflecting critically on these points – giving my own opinions on the article as a whole.

The first category they look at is geographies of access and enablement – an exploration into who has access to technology, and peoples participation and representation in a digitally connected space. Here they show that most of the internet population, the number of people in a country who have internet access, reside in Asia and North America. More so, it can be seen that while countries in the Global North have quite large internet populations and penetration, the Global South (Africa in particular) hardly has any internet penetration. This can be accounted to what they label as one of the core themes of the global digital divide – broadband cost. While North America may seemingly have high broadband costs, relative to the countries average income they are extremely low. In contrast to this, broadband costs in African countries tend to be extremely high relative to their average income, with one of the worst being Eritrea’s yearly broadband cost which equates to 50 years of average income. This shows how there is ultimately an uneven access of information between the Global North and Global South because of relative broadband costs.

With those differences now in mind, we can then start to look at why websites need both a good UI and UX. From the above we can see that UI and UX serve two different purposes, however if a website only accommodates one, or the other, it would result in bad web design. This is because both the way a user interacts with a website, and what that website looks like, play a part in keeping a good userbase. If a website had an extremely beautiful design that distracted users from performing basic tasks, then users would become frustrated and not want to use the site again. On the other end, if a website is overly functional, and contains no visuals, then the user will not actually enjoy using the website. As a result, UI and UX designers have to work together to create a balance of the two in order to please their users in the hopes of creating a consistent userbase.

Thereafter, the authors discuss geographies of participation, which looks at where digital information is generated. To do this they focus on three areas: domain names, GitHub users, and Wikipedia edits. Across these different categories of participation, the authors noted some interesting trends. Firstly, they found that 93% of all domain address registered on the internet come from Global North, with only 2% of the remaining 7% being registered in Africa and the Middle East. Secondly, when looking at the distribution of collaborative coding on GitHub they found that North America and Europe both over produced relative to the userbase of GitHub in that country. Thirdly, they found that while Wikipedia does allow anyone to edit and contribute, there is a problem with who provides the information about different countries – with less than 5% of all content posted about Africa actually being locally contributed to. All of this highlights the difference in informational geography and participation between the Global North and Global South, with a particular emphasis on the lack of participation from the African continent. This in turn shows the inherent bias of information because of this skew in contributions, resulting in information becoming questionable.

Next, there is the category of geographies of representation – which is all about who content is created for and who it is created by. To analyse this, they look at various different digital representations in order to understand spatial differences – starting with Google Searches. By using Google to search every countries name in various languages, it can be seen that there is definitely a skew in the mentions of countries compared to their internet population. This can clearly be seen through the sheer number of mentions of the United States, Japan, China, and the United Kingdom compared to their internet population. Thereafter, they looked at OpenStreetMap, which is a freely editable map. Here they found that North America, in combination with four other European countries, accounted for over 58% of all the content on the platform. Lastly, they looked at two important databases to the semantic web – Freebase and Geonames. While both are looked at independently in the journal article, we are able to combine the outcomes from both as they show very similar results. Both Freebase and Geonames show North America and Europe having proportionally large representation when compared to other countries. All of this again ultimately shows the power of, and skew of information from, the Global North – with a particular amount of power given to North America and Europe, and a lack of power in Africa.

Now, with this journal article summarised I can start to reflect on it as a whole. From this article, it can be seen that there is indeed still a discrepancy in the geography of information between the Global North and South, and while it was believed that technologies such as the web would help close this gap, this ultimately proved to be too idealistic. In fact, it is extremely easy to see the effect of this in our daily life. When accessing any social media or news site in South Africa, you would be surprised to see that it is inundated with American and European information, with local content and information mostly taking second stage. Not only is this problematic from the perspective of the information we consume, but it is also problematic as it makes it harder for local content to be shared – ultimately shaping the Global North as more powerful in the digital space.

However, while I do agree with the outcome of the journal article, I believe it is fundamentally flawed in some aspects. This is because the journal article was written from the perspective of the western Global North, and as such has its own geographic information bias – with both Graham and De Sabbata from the United Kingdom, and Zook from the United States. The biggest problem that stems from this is that the journal article ignores any region specific services and only focuses on what the western Global North has deemed as widely acceptable. An example of this can be seen when looking at China, as they have a huge internet population but in every other category and section they pale in comparison to the rest of the Global North in representation. This is most likely due to China having their own Intranet, and suite of applications, that most of their population is accessing. So while I do understand that this journal article is only giving a preliminary look at data, it failes to address information like this, and potential other exclusions and oversights because of the specific dataset used. This can result in people ignoring the actual outcome of the paper, which I mostly agree with, and viewing it purely as another paper written only from the perspective of the Global North.
