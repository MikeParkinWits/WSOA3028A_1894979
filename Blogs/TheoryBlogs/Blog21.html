What is Dark UX?

By Michael Parkin | 8 June 2021 | Words: 557 | Theory

User experience (UX) revolves around the end user’s interactions and experience with a product. As such, in web design it is extremely important to understand the way the end user interacts with your site, to design a good UX. However, this understanding of the user’s interaction can also be used for malicious intent, through dark UX patterns. In this blog post I then delve further into dark UX, first looking at what it is and its origins, before then going over the different types and the impact they have.

To start, it is important that we understand what dark UX patterns are, and the history behind them. The term dark UX pattern was first coined by Harry Brignull in 2010, where he defines it as “a user interface carefully crafted to trick users into doing things they might not otherwise do” (Brignull, 2013). While Brignull uses this definition to refer to digital dark UX patterns, it is important to understand that the use of dark UX patterns predates the internet, with examples of this seen in museums that require you exit through the gift store. Although, Brignull also notes that there is a difference between dark UX and bad UX – where UX is not considered at all, and thus there is no malicious intent.

With dark UX patterns now defined, we can then start to look at the different types of dark UX patterns. Brignull outlines 12 different types of dark patterns: trick questions, sneak into basket, roach motel, privacy suckering, price comparison prevention, misdirection, hidden costs, bait and switch, confirm shaming, disguised ads, force continuity, and friend spam. However, for the sake of this blog post I will just be looking at the three most common types – starting with trick questions.

Brignull defines trick questions as when you are filling out a form and “you respond to a question, which, when glanced upon quickly appears to ask one thing, but if read carefully, asks another thing entirely” (Birgnull, N/A). Common ways this is done is when a platform subverts the meaning of a check box, where ticking it means no. Next there is forced continuity. On the internet, many services are paid. As a result, users often sign up for free trials, most of which require a credit card. Forced continuity then comes into play when this service continues to charge your credit card every month, without warning – ultimately abusing the lack of rules around this.

The last major dark pattern type I will speak about is misdirection. This occurs when a user’s focus is directed to one place, in order to draw attention away from another space. An example of this can be seen on sites where content appears to be locked behind a paywall, while upon further investigation the content is just present elsewhere on the page.

Ultimately, by looking at just two examples of dark UX patterns, we can see how powerful UX design is. More specifically, we can see how UX design can be used maliciously to lead users to performing various actions which they never intended, all without their knowledge. As such, it is important that designers have an understanding of these design patterns so that they can avoid them, and actively design a better user experiences.
